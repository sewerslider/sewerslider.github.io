On systemd (p1)

A word about systemd

So right now I’m typing this on artix. I have installed artix, and reinstall arch and vice versa ad neauseam but everytime, I just can’t stop thinking about reaping systemd out of my system. Suffice to say, systemd bugged me a lot. There’s things about it always rubs me the wrong way. And that’s why I write this - to try to get the thoughts out and maybe convince some of you to at least consider a systemd-free distro

My way or the highway

So systemd first started out as an init system. And later on, It tries to replace a lot of the common utilities one would use on a linux desktop (bootloader, Network manager, DNS manager, etc). Now this would be all well and good (I think the GNU coreutils are really good software) except for 1 problems: Tight coupling. Unlike the GNU coreutils, which is basically just a bunch of software that is grouped with each others, systemd programs are hard linked to each others (i.e they can’t work seperately). This leads to another problem, and In my opinion the most problematic part of systemd

The codebase

Due to all utilities being mingled together, every systemd component basically become a part of the systemd codebase, thus making it big, complex and fragile. This is made worse by the fact that systemd (and all of its component due to the tight coupling) now run as PID 1, and if any of them have a failure, there’s a chance your system will crash.

You know, I find it funny that there’re people who make fun of Xorg because you can perform a DOS on a local Xorg server while unironically defend systemd - a tool that can effectively ddos a machine that happens to use it

On an unrelated note, I feel like Redhat had learned nothing from the time they maintain Xorg. They constantly rant about how it is so big and complex while failing to realize that those same words can be used to describe systemd. I think systemd will be the next X11/Xorg: a few years from now, Redhat will realize that they can’t maintain systemd anymore. They will migrate to a new system and anyone who can’t (their customers even) will be met with arrogance when they beg redhat to not drop support for it. Maybe I’m wrong here. Maybe this time they will be able to maintain it. But I have lost faith in them.

With all that said, I’m not saying I will never use a systemd distro. I might well use one in the future, on 1 conditions: They have something to justify their choice of init. If they use it, they must have a reason that’s better than “it’s what everyone is using”.

Say, I might have been a little negative in this writing. In truth, I don’t hate systemd all that much. I did use it in the past and from that experience, I don’t have too much to hate about it. I think even if I dislike systemd, I would still try it again and dig deeper just to see why everyone use it rather than smaller alternatives like runit or s6
